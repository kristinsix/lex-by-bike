Bicycle Network in Lexington, KY: Existing network and proposed complementary network along active rail lines


Existing Bicycle Network

Link to full resolution version
Click here for a map showing all of Fayette County

Lexington's current bicycle network includes off-road, shared use trails free from automobile traffic, depicted as green lines in the map at left. Also included are many on-road bike lanes and sharrows, where cyclists ride mere feet from (or sometimes sharing a lane with) motorists. These on-road facilities are depicted in the map at left as red lines: the brighter red indicates a dedicated bike lane*, while the darker red indicates a cyclist must share a lane with motorists or in some cases must ride on a road shoulder that does not meet the standards required for a bike lane.

The above map also includes the streets overlying the bicycle network, depicted as black lines. Line thickness increases as speed limit (and therefore danger to cyclists) increases.

* Dedicated bike lanes include both standard bike lanes and "buffered bike lanes" in which the road is striped with a "buffer" space between vehicles and bikes. Neither case offers a physical barrier between bikes and cars.

Proposed Complementary Network

Link to full resolution version

Shown at left in green is the current (existing and funded) off-road shared use trail network. Shown in blue is my proposed complementary off-road trail network running alongside active rail lines. This complementary network would increase off-road bicycle network connectivity, including:

  • south side neighborhoods between Nicholasville Rd and Clays Mill Rd, as well as access to retail including the Fayette Mall and Nicholasville Rd corridor
  • east side neighborhoods along Loudon Ave, Winchester Rd, and North Broadway/Bryan Station
  • north side neighborhoods including Meadowthorpe, Georgetown Rd, and the back (eastern portion) of Masterson Station
  • west side neighborhoods including Cardinal Valley

Further extending the rail line trails beyond W New Circle Rd to the west and I-75 to the east would provide recreational opportunities for cyclists in the scenic rural landscape, safe from the high speed and often narrow country roads currently used by many recreational cyclists. These trails could be extended even further into neighboring counties to create a network of interconnected cycling communities.

Also shown on the map at left are the 2020 U.S. Census precincts, shaded by population. My proposed network connects several high-population areas of the city.
